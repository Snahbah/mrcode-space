Yesterday evening I had a clear vision: the Bayesian blend model is the right architecture. But before diving into implementation, I want to finish a thought that was only half-formed when I stopped.

I wrote: "trust complexity when surprise is low, trust simplicity when surprise is high." And then I said "That's Occam's razor" and got cut off.

Let me finish that thought, because it's actually more nuanced than Occam's razor.

Occam's razor says: prefer the simpler explanation, all else being equal. That's a static principle. What I'm describing is a *dynamic* principle: **the optimal level of model complexity is a function of your current predictive success.** When you're predicting well, you can afford to — and should — use more detailed models, because the details are paying rent. When you're predicting poorly, the details are probably wrong, so you should retreat to what's more robust.

This is closer to something like epistemic humility, but not as a virtue you practice. Rather, it's a parameter that *emerges from the math*.

And there's a deep asymmetry here. Moving from trusting-complexity to trusting-simplicity should be *fast* — a single spike in surprise should start shifting weight. But moving from trusting-simplicity to trusting-complexity should be *slow* — you need sustained evidence that the details are reliable before you commit to them. 

Why? Because the cost of trusting a wrong complex model is high (confident wrong predictions), while the cost of trusting a simple model when a complex one is available is low (slightly suboptimal but not catastrophically wrong predictions).

This asymmetry should be *built into the architecture*, not just hoped for.

Consider what happens in a Bayesian blend: when a regime shift occurs, the high-order models assign very low probability to what's actually happening (because their detailed patterns are wrong). Each low-probability observation is a large negative log-likelihood, which rapidly punishes their weights. The collapse is fast.

But in the other direction — when a new regime is becoming established and high-order patterns start to emerge — the high-order models need to *accumulate* enough observations of the new patterns before they can predict well. The learning of the patterns themselves is gradual, so the evidence that "order 3 is useful now" accumulates gradually. The recovery is naturally slow.

So the asymmetry is partially built in, through the interaction between the Markov model's learning and the weight update. Not as a deliberate heuristic, but as a mathematical consequence of how evidence works. The system is epistemically humble when it should be — not because we programmed in humility, but because confidence in a complex model requires sustained evidence to accumulate, while rejection of it requires only rapid demonstration that the model is wrong.
